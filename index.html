ARE CHARITABLE CHILDREN INSTITUTIONS INDISPENSABLE IN PROTECTION OF CHILDREN IN NEED OF CARE AND PROTECTION IN KENYA?

The position of Kenya with regard to children rights prior to 1989 was aligned with the common law perspective that children did not need any special protection since they were deemed to be sufficiently protected as part of the family. This position changed with the ratification of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child in 1989 and the African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child in 1990, both of which Kenya adopted without reservations in 1990 and 2000 respectively.

Pursuant to the provisions of Article 1 of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child and Article 4 African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child, Kenya enacted the Children Act No 8 of 2001 in order to give effect to the provisions of the two treaties. By extension the Act also intended to provide for the care and protection of children and the administration of children institutions.

Pursuant to the provisions of Article 1 of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child and Article 4 African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child, Kenya enacted the Children Act No 8 of 2001 in order to give effect to the provisions of the two treaties. By extension the Act also intended to provide for the care and protection of children and the administration of children institutions.

The Act provides that a child in need of care and protection includes a child who has been abandoned by their parents; one is found begging; who is prevented from receiving education; a female child likely to be subjected to harmful cultural practices; one who is exposed to domestic violence; one who is pregnant; one who has or is likely to be sexually abused and also one who is involved in the use or trafficking of harmful substances, among others.

In furtherance of this duty to protect children in need of care and protection, the 2001 Children Act provided for the establishment of Charitable Children Institutions. These were meant to be tools to assist the State safeguard the interests of children. They are defined under the Act as a home or institution, whether established a person, corporate, religious organization or Non-governmental Organization that has the approval of the council to provide care, protection, rehabilitation or control of children.

Since the commencement of the Children Act No. 18 of 2001, in 2002 there has been a proliferation of Charitable Children Institutions around the country to the extent that the government took notice. These Institutions are key in the protection of these vulnerable children considering that the State would likely find it hard to protect all the children in need of care and protection on it’s own.

In a recent report, by the National Crime Research Center and the State Department for Children Services, it was noted that all categories listed under Section 119 of the Children Act of 2001, were admitted in Charitable Children Institutions around the country. The percentage of the children admitted under each category varied, but the highest rates were noted among children facing neglect; orphaned children; sexual abuse and extreme poverty at home.

The Charitable Children Institutions take various measures in safeguarding the safety of children in need of care and protection. Some of these measures include: providing education, spiritual growth, guidance and counselling, shelter and psychological support. The provision of these services are crucial for the physical, mental and spiritual wellbeing of any child, and the intervention by such Charitable Children Institutions give even the most vulnerable of children a chance at a good start in life.

However as with all things, where the there is light there is also darkness. In this case, some Charitable Children Institutions have morphed into cartels and propagators of the very crimes they claim to prevent. For instance, some of the crimes reported range from child labour, caning and pinching of children, emotional abuse as well as sexual assault. This is extremely worrisome considering that some of the children in such Institutions have already gone through these very crimes, and were there for protection.

This calls for a clear investigation of the merits and demerits of the institutionalization of children in need of care and protection in Kenya. This because any rushed decision, will only yield these vulnerable children as casualties. If all the Charitable Children Institutions are all closed, what happens to all the children already depending on them now and in the near future, can the government bear the burden? On the other hand, if the status quo remains as it is, what will be of the children facing double victimization at the hands of their supposed saviors?
